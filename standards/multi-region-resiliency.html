Scope

In Scope

  • Chaos engineering and resilience testing practices

Out of Scope

Requirements (7)

Requirements use RFC 2119 severity levels.

REQ-001 MUST

Tier 1 services MUST deploy in active-active configuration across a minimum of 2 geographic regions.

Active-active multi-region deployment eliminates single points of failure at the regional level, ensuring service continuity during regional outages or disasters.

Verification: Architecture review confirms multi-region deployment topology; monitoring dashboards validate traffic distribution across regions.
Platforms: AWS, Azure, GCP
Tiers: Tier 1, Tier 2
REQ-002 MUST NOT

Recovery Time Objective (RTO) MUST NOT exceed 15 minutes for Tier 1 services.

An RTO of 15 minutes or less minimizes business impact during regional failures and meets enterprise SLA commitments to customers.

Verification: Quarterly disaster recovery tests measure actual recovery time; results are recorded and tracked against the 15-minute threshold.
Platforms: AWS, Azure, GCP
Tiers: Tier 1, Tier 2
REQ-003 MUST

Automated failover MUST be tested quarterly via chaos engineering exercises.

Regular chaos engineering exercises validate that failover mechanisms work as designed and identify weaknesses before real incidents occur.

Verification: Chaos engineering exercise reports are submitted quarterly; automated failover success rate is tracked per service.
Platforms: AWS, Azure, GCP
Tiers: Tier 1, Tier 2
REQ-004 MUST

Data replication MUST achieve a Recovery Point Objective (RPO) of less than 1 minute for Tier 1 services.

Sub-minute RPO ensures minimal data loss during regional failures, protecting critical business transactions and maintaining data integrity.

Verification: Replication lag monitoring alerts when RPO exceeds threshold; disaster recovery tests validate actual data loss window.
Platforms: AWS, Azure, GCP
Tiers: Tier 1, Tier 2
REQ-005 SHOULD

Health check endpoints SHOULD implement standardized liveness and readiness probes.

Standardized health checks enable consistent monitoring, automated traffic routing decisions, and faster incident detection across all regions.

Verification: Service health check endpoints are validated against the standardized schema during deployment pipeline checks.
Platforms: AWS, Azure, GCP
Tiers: Tier 1, Tier 2
REQ-006 SHOULD

Services SHOULD implement graceful degradation patterns for non-critical dependencies.

Graceful degradation ensures that failures in non-critical downstream services do not cascade and cause complete service outages.

Verification: Chaos engineering tests inject failures in non-critical dependencies and verify that core service functionality remains available.
Platforms: AWS, Azure, GCP
Tiers: Tier 1, Tier 2
REQ-007 MUST

Disaster recovery runbooks MUST be maintained and reviewed after each incident.

Up-to-date runbooks reduce mean time to recovery by providing clear, tested procedures for incident response teams.

Verification: Runbook review timestamps are checked post-incident; runbook completeness is audited during quarterly resilience reviews.
Platforms: AWS, Azure, GCP
Tiers: Tier 1, Tier 2

Guidelines

GUIDE-001

Chaos engineering exercises should follow the principles of progressive blast radius expansion. Start with small-scale fault injections in non-production environments before advancing to production. Use tools like Chaos Monkey or Litmus to automate experiments and ensure reproducibility.

GUIDE-002

Circuit breaker patterns should be configured with appropriate thresholds for failure rate, slow call rate, and recovery timeout. Recommended defaults are 50% failure rate threshold, 10-second sliding window, and 30-second half-open recovery period.

GUIDE-003

Disaster recovery runbooks should be stored alongside service documentation and include step-by-step failover procedures, rollback instructions, communication templates, and escalation paths. Runbooks should be tested during each quarterly chaos engineering exercise.

Definitions

TermDefinition
Recovery Time Objective (RTO) The maximum acceptable amount of time that a service can be offline or unavailable after a failure or disaster before business impact becomes unacceptable.
Recovery Point Objective (RPO) The maximum acceptable amount of data loss measured in time. An RPO of 1 minute means at most 1 minute of data may be lost during a failure event.
Active-Active A deployment topology where two or more regions simultaneously serve production traffic, with each region capable of handling the full workload independently if other regions become unavailable.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between active-active and active-passive multi-region deployment?
In active-active, all regions serve live traffic simultaneously, providing lower latency and immediate failover. In active-passive, the secondary region is on standby and only activated during a failover event, resulting in higher RTO but potentially simpler data consistency management.
How do we handle data consistency across regions in an active-active setup?
Use conflict-free replicated data types (CRDTs) or last-writer-wins strategies for eventually consistent data. For strong consistency requirements, designate a primary region for writes and use synchronous replication with consensus protocols. The choice depends on the service's CAP theorem trade-offs.
Are Tier 2 services required to implement active-active multi-region deployment?
Tier 2 services are not required to implement active-active deployment but SHOULD follow the standard where feasible. At minimum, Tier 2 services must have a documented disaster recovery plan with defined RTO and RPO targets appropriate to their business criticality.
